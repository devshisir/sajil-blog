How to Boost a Stagnant Credit Score

Credit scores are essential ratings that allow us to apply for loans, enjoy optimal interest rates, and prove to lenders that we are reliable with our money.

However, sometimes our credit scores drop, and it can seem like an uphill battle to bring them back to good and excellent levels.

Thankfully, you can apply some simple strategies to your personal finances that can quickly boost your credit score.

Whether you are looking to boost your credit score to apply for better rates, or you want to achieve the next level in your personal finances, taking the time to increase your credit score will pay dividends and help you feel more confident with your money.

Understand your score

Once you have decided to pursue a higher credit score, the first step is understanding why your score is not as high as you would like it to be.

There are many resources from reputable companies that will offer comprehensive credit checks for free.

Banks, utility companies, property managers, and other lenders who require credit checks submit data to all of their customers and tenants' credit bureaus.

IThis data is compiled and given to you when you file a free credit report.

Credit reports usually have a breakdown of your credit score's various components whether you use VantageScore, FICO, or another type of credit score.

Your credit report will help you gauge where you need the most help.

Delinquencies, high balances, and an abundance of hard credit inquiries can all drag your credit score down, but if you don't know where to start, you'll be aiming at a moving target.

Thankfully, free credit reports are widely available and help you identify your weak spots before taking any further action.

Dispute any inaccuracies on the report

If you see accounts that you don't own or delinquencies that are not true on your credit report, file a complaint at once and report them to the credit bureau.

The big three credit bureaus, Experian, Equifax, and Transunion, Should all have the same data. If there are any erroneous accounts or inaccuracies on your report, they can drag your credit score down.

Resolving these inaccuracies would likely result in a quick boost to your score.

Pay Your Bills

Paying your bills on time, and prioritizing your payments is a great way to raise your score.

Service and utility providers report delinquencies directly to the credit bureaus, and if you have missed payments are some of your bills, reach out to your service provider and see if they would be willing to work with you to help you get caught up and stop reporting delinquencies to the credit bureaus.

Suppose you can catch up on your bills quickly. In that case, it will have a compound impact on your credit score because an abundance of positive activity on delinquent accounts will accelerate your score's recovery.

Avoid too many hard inquiries

When you apply for a new loan, the lender makes a hard check on your credit history.

Hard checks always drop your score a few points, but it will usually bounce right back after a month or two.

However, suppose you are in the habit of applying for new credit cards and Loans often. In that case, this will show on your credit report as a negative impact because you may be a riskier borrower if you frequently need access to new lines of credit.

Before applying for new credit, ask yourself if you really need it. Chances are there are other Solutions or offers that don't require a hard credit inquiry.

If you can stop applying for new credit for a few months, you should rapidly see an increase in your credit score, And hard inquiries will disappear entirely after two years.

Make Credit Utilization Your Friend

Credit utilization is a huge component of your credit score. Your credit utilization is the ratio of how much credit you are utilizing versus how much you have available.

For example, if you have a credit card with a limit of $10,000 and your balance is $5,000, your credit ratio would be 50%.

The optimal credit utilization ratio is 30% or less, but thankfully there are a couple of solutions to negate the impact of a high credit utilization ratio.

Ask for higher limits

If you are relatively stable and your personal finances, but you keep running into the issue of a high credit utilization ratio, you should be able to ask your bank or lending company or higher limits on your lines of credit.

The goal is not to use the higher limit; instead, it will lower your utilization ratio.

If we refer to our example above, raising your limit from $10,000 to $20,000 will drop your utilization ratio from 50% to 25%, which is in line with an average utilization ratio.

Keep Your Cards Open

Another way to boost your score is by keeping old credit cards open instead of closing them when you stop using them.

As long as there are no annual fees or interest payments, these old credit cards factor into your overall available credit.

If you close the accounts, your credit utilization ratio will jump because you removed thousands of dollars of available credit from your accounts.

Track Your Progress

It's been said, "you can't manage what you can't measure" and this especially applies to your credit score.

Once you start taking steps to improve your overall credit score, get in the habit of regular credit check-ups for your accounts.

It wouldn't make sense to do all the hard work to improve your credit score, only to let your efforts slip away.

Many companies, apps, and services are freely available to help you monitor your credit score and offer tools to optimize your finances.
