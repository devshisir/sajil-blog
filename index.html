Introduction to IRA's - Traditional, Roth, or both?

Saving money and investing for retirement is a crucial component of personal finance. Understanding how retirement accounts fit into your plan can alleviate a substantial amount of worry on your journey to and throughout your golden years. When it comes to IRAs, there can be some confusion between Traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs, the two most common. The primary difference focuses on when the money in the accounts is taxed, but there are other nuances. Once you understand how the two types of IRAs operate, you should integrate them into your retirement saving strategy.

When Should You Start Saving for Retirement?

There is some debate around retirement planning, but there is unanimous agreement that you should start investing and saving for retirement as soon as you can.
Compound interest is the silver bullet that helps wealth accumulate over time.

When you start investing, the returns you generate will be added to your account balance, and future returns will cause that number to grow.

Individual Retirement Accounts

Since saving for retirement is so important, where should you start?

There are many options on the market, but the two most common choices are employer-sponsored accounts like 401ks and Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs).

The most significant difference between 401ks and IRAs is that your employer provides the 401k in their benefits package and may offer to match a portion of your contributions. If you leave the company, you can roll the balance into your personal IRA since that is not attached to any outside entity.

This brings us to an important distinction between traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs.

Pre vs. Post Tax

IRAs are subject to taxes like other investment products, but there is a crucial difference between when the taxes are paid.

Traditional IRAs are considered pre-tax vehicles, which means your contributions and capital gains are not taxed until you begin withdrawing money from your account during retirement.

The advantage of deferring taxes until later is that you can contribute a more considerable amount of pre-tax dollars initially, which can lead to higher compounding.

Post-tax contributions fund Roth IRAs. The money invested through a Roth account is already taxed, so all gains and withdrawals during retirement are tax-free.

The motivation for this after-tax account stems from the assumption that you will likely retire in a higher tax bracket than you currently are in.

This is a significant tax advantage because by essentially prepaying the taxes at a lower rate, you can potentially save thousands of dollars over the long run.

Contribution Limits

Since both IRA structures offer powerful incentives to investors, they each have annual contribution limits and income caps to enjoy full benefits.

Traditional

Traditional IRAs have an annual contribution limit of $6,000 if you are under 50 years old and $7,000 if you are 50 or older to help you "catch up."

While there is no income limit for contributing to a Traditional IRA, your contributions may not be tax-deductible if your income is too high.

Roth

Roth IRAs have the same contribution limits as the Traditional IRA of $6,000 if you are under 50 years old and $7,000 if you are 50 or older.

However, there is an income limit for contributions of $140,000 for individuals and $208,000 for married couples.

Withdrawal Rules

Whether you invest in a Traditional or a Roth IRA, you are eligible to withdraw funds at the age of 59½.

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Under the rules for a Traditional IRA, you must begin taking distributions from your account when you reach age 72. The amount of the distribution is determined by your income and tax rate during retirement.

There are no RMDs for Roth accounts because the taxes were paid at the time you contributed to the account.

Early Withdrawal Penalties

What if you want to withdraw money from an IRA before you turn 59½?

If you take an early withdrawal from a Traditional IRA, you will immediately pay a 10% penalty, along with taxes at your current rate.

There are certain exceptions where the penalty can be waived, such as becoming disabled, if a spouse receives your account in the event of your death, or cove qualifying expenses such as education or adoption expenses.

You can withdraw any contributions you made to a Roth IRA without paying taxes or penalties, which is called a qualified distribution. However, starting any earnings generated by the account have different rules which can impact your ability to take the money out.

A Powerful Combination

You may be able to enjoy the benefits of both Traditional and Roth IRAs, depending on your current situation.

If you are earlier in your career, a Roth account's after-tax advantages can help you secure serious tax savings over your investment's growth.

As you progress in your career, the pre-tax benefits of a Traditional IRA can accelerate your returns' growth before you retire.
